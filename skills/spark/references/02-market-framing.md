# Market Framing — Entry Point Over Inflated TAM

Do not start with "The global XYZ market is $50B."
Start with: "Who, right now, in a narrow segment, will actually pay for this?"

## Core Questions

```
- Is this a known problem or a newly created one?
- Who already pays to solve it?
- Is this urgent, recurring, or optional?
- Is there a narrow segment where this hurts enough to buy?
- Is this venture-scale, lifestyle-scale, or unclear?
```

## Label Every Claim

Use three labels for factual precision:

* `[confirmed]` — You have receipts. Customer interviews, pricing docs, contract terms.
* `[estimated]` — Reasonable assumption based on market research or similar products.
* `[unverified]` — Educated guess. Needs validation.

**Do not present guesses as facts.** If you can't label it, you don't understand it yet.

## Working Through Market Framing

### Known vs. Created Problem

**Known problem examples:**
- "B2B companies waste time on expense reporting." (Solved by Expensify, Brex, etc. — but imperfectly.)
- "E-commerce sellers manually copy product listings to 5 marketplaces." (Real, expensive, recurring.)

**Created problem examples:**
- "People want to see their favorite creator's face while they shop." (Nice to have. Are they paying? Unlikely.)
- "Enterprises need ML for X." (Maybe they don't. Did they ask?)

If you invented the problem, you have a harder task: education before sales.

### Willing Payer

The magic filter:

Does anyone **today** pay money for a solution to this problem?

If yes → Look at their alternatives and pricing. You have a market floor.
If no → You need customer development first. Product work is premature.

### Urgency Spectrum

| Urgency | Examples | Revenue Lift |
|---------|----------|------|
| **Urgent** | "Servers are down" (ops), "Compliance deadline Friday" (legal) | Pay premium, buy fast |
| **Recurring** | "Every employee spends 6 hours/week on this" (operations), "Payroll clerks review timesheets daily" (HR) | Predictable, easier to sell |
| **Optional** | "Nice to have", "Would be convenient", "Aspirational" | Lowest priority, lowest budget |

Where does your idea sit? If it's optional, your wedge must be elsewhere (e.g., brand, lock-in, network effects).

### Narrow Entry Segment

Don't say: "SMBs and enterprises both need this."
Say: "Shoe retailers with 5-25 stores, who already use Shopify and Slack, and have an ops manager role."

The narrower the segment, the more precise your messaging, the faster you validate.

### Venture vs. Lifestyle vs. Unclear

**Venture-scale:**
- Large TAM (>$1B), high margin opportunity, winner-take-most dynamics.
- Example: "Payment infrastructure for emerging markets" (Stripe-like).

**Lifestyle-scale:**
- Profitable for founder(s), low growth ambition, defensible niche.
- Example: "DIY bookkeeping templates for freelancers" ($100K/year revenue, no VC).

**Unclear:**
- You haven't decided. That's fine. Say so. Your next action is to clarify the business model.

## Red Flags

- TAM numbers without a stated entry segment → Overstated
- "Everyone needs this" with no buyer persona → No one will
- Confusing "problem exists" with "customer will pay to fix it" → Two different things

# Competitor Scan — Look at What People Actually Use

Competitor scan is not "who else has the same headline."
It's "what are people actually doing right now to solve this problem?"

## The Six-Layer Scan

### 1. Direct Competitors

Who sells a product for the same use case?

Examples:
- Expensify, Brex, Bill.com for expense/payment automation
- Figma, Adobe XD, Sketch for design collaboration

Look at: pricing, features, customer reviews, G2 sentiment, years in market.

### 2. Indirect Substitutes

Products that solve the same problem differently.

Examples:
- For "design collaboration": Figma, Adobe XD, Miro, even Slack threads
- For "payroll automation": ADP, Gusto, custom SAP setup, external accountants

Ask: Why do customers pick one over the other?

### 3. Incumbent Workflows

What are people doing *right now*, even if it's not a product?

Examples:
- Manually copying product listings to 5 marketplaces (the real competitor: manual labor)
- Using Google Sheets to track expenses (the competitor: free, already paid for)
- Hiring a VA to manage customer emails (the competitor: outsourcing)

The **incumbents** are often the strongest competitors because they're already embedded in operations.

### 4. Spreadsheets / Agencies / Internal Teams

"We built it ourselves" or "We hired someone to do it."

Ask customers:
- How many people touch this process per week?
- What does it cost in salary + tools?
- What breaks when that person leaves?

If the internal cost is <$50K/year, you need a low-price solution.
If it's >$200K/year (e.g., full-time analyst), you have pricing room.

### 5. "Good Enough" Alternatives

Products that solve 80% of the problem for 20% of the price.

Examples:
- Mailchimp for email vs. Klaviyo (Mailchimp is good enough for many SMBs)
- Airtable for workflows vs. SAP (Airtable is good enough for startups)

These are the hardest competitors because they're cheap, familiar, and adequate.

### 6. Do Nothing / Tolerate the Pain

The default alternative: **not solving it at all.**

Many problems aren't painful enough to warrant a purchase. Customers live with them.

You must find segments where the cost of the problem exceeds the cost of your solution.

## Four Critical Questions

### Why Would Someone Switch?

What would make a customer leave their incumbent (spreadsheet, agency, existing tool) for your product?

Rank by power:
1. **Switching cost too high** (most powerful lock-in) → You need massive improvement
2. **Good enough** → You need lower price or dramatically faster implementation
3. **Incumbent lags behind** → You can compete on features, UX, speed
4. **New use case incumbent can't serve** → Easiest to win (low switching cost)

### What Is the Switching Cost?

Real costs customers bear when moving:

- Data migration complexity
- Retraining time
- Risk of disruption during transition
- Contract lock-in
- Integration effort
- Psychological "we've always done it this way"

Low switching cost = easier to acquire. High switching cost = need much stronger product or price advantage.

### What's the Easiest Feature for a Competitor to Copy?

If a larger competitor (Salesforce, Google, Microsoft) can build your top 3 features in 6 months, you don't have a moat.

Think about defensibility:
- **Network effects** (hard to copy, but rare at launch)
- **Data moat** (hard to copy if you own the dataset)
- **Switching costs** (hard to copy if deeply embedded)
- **Brand** (hard to copy, slow to build)
- **Integrations** (hard to copy if you own the ecosystem)

If your defense is "we're smarter," you're not defensible. Your competitors are smart too.

### Where Is the Wedge Actually Defensible?

Pick one narrow use case where you are harder to displace than a generalist.

Examples:
- **Stripe** (payments): Started with the hardest use case (high-volume, international). Competitors couldn't easily replicate the engineering.
- **Slack** (team chat): Started in gaming (real-time, latency-sensitive). Chose a wedge where existing tools (email, IRC) were clearly broken.
- **Figma** (design): Started with web-first multiplayer. Harder to copy than single-player tools.

## Scan Template

| Layer | Player | Features | Price | Lock-In | Why Customers Use It | Why They Might Leave |
|-------|--------|----------|-------|---------|----------------------|----------------------|
| Direct | Expensify | OCR, workflows, integrations | $8/user/mo | Moderate | Easy receipt capture | Cost, overkill for simple needs |
| Indirect | Brex | Corporate card + expense tool | 3% fee | High | All-in-one, cash back | Not for freelancers, minimum spend |
| Incumbent | Google Sheets | Free, flexible, familiar | $0 | Very high | Already know it, free | Manual, slow, error-prone |
| DIY | Internal team | Custom, exact fit | Salary | N/A | Solves specific edge cases | Dependent on one person |
| Good Enough | Wave | Free, basic automation | $0 | Low | Free, simple | Weak reporting, limited integrations |

## Red Flags

- No competitors (incumbent or direct) → The problem might not exist
- All competitors are VC-funded, $500M+ raises → Venture market, high barrier to entry
- Competitors dominating through lock-in only → You need to break lock-in, not match features
- No clear wedge where you're stronger → You're making a "better" product in a crowded space (hard)

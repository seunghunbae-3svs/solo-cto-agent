# Scenarios — Three Believable Paths

Do not build one forecast. Build a few believable paths based on different assumptions.

This is where weak ideas often become visible. If you can't articulate a plausible upside, you don't have a business.

## The Three Scenarios

### Best Case: What Goes Unusually Right?

This is not "we dominate the world."
This is: "What if the three biggest risks don't materialize, and we execute well?"

Set of assumptions:
- Sales velocity is 2x expected (market is hungrier than anticipated)
- Churn is lower than assumed (product is more sticky)
- Expansion revenue is higher (customers use more than expected)
- CAC is lower (viral growth, partnerships, PR lower acquisition cost)
- Gross margin is higher (economies of scale kick in faster)

**Example Best Case:**

Scenario: Expense management for mid-market companies ($100M–$1B revenue)

Assumptions:
```
Year 1:  10 customers, $120K ARR
Year 2:  40 customers (3.5x growth), $800K ARR
Year 3:  120 customers (3x growth), $3.5M ARR
Churn:   2% monthly (low, because CFOs lock in)
CAC:     $2K (sales motion is lightweight, partner referrals)
LTV:     $45K (high expansion, strong retention)
```

**Narrative:** "We nail the product-market fit with mid-market ops teams. They pay fast, churn slowly, and expand into the finance team. Partnership with Salesforce for channel distribution accelerates growth."

### Base Case: What Happens If This Is Merely Decent?

This is your most likely scenario. Not spectacular. Not terrible. Competent execution against real market demand.

Assumptions:
- Sales velocity matches reasonable market expectations
- Churn is in line with category (e.g., 4–6% for SMB SaaS)
- CAC is reasonable but not low
- Some customers expand, some churn
- Gross margin is solid but not exceptional

**Example Base Case:**

Same expense management product.

Assumptions:
```
Year 1:  8 customers, $90K ARR
Year 2:  20 customers (2.5x growth), $500K ARR
Year 3:  50 customers (2.5x growth), $2M ARR
Churn:   5% monthly
CAC:     $4K
LTV:     $18K
```

**Narrative:** "We get traction with mid-market ops. Growth is steady but requires consistent sales effort. We remain a focused solution, not a category killer."

### Failure Case: What Breaks First?

This is the realism check. If you can't name what kills the idea, you haven't thought hard enough.

Name one (or more) single point of failure:

Examples:
- **Problem not painful enough:** Customers say "it would be nice to have" but don't prioritize budget
- **Customer will not pay:** The buyer exists but no one has budget authority
- **Switching cost too high:** Current tools are embedded in workflows; migration is a nightmare
- **Incumbent can copy instantly:** Salesforce/Microsoft sees your wedge and builds it in 6 months
- **Distribution is harder than product:** You build the product but can't acquire customers at reasonable CAC
- **Product only works in theory:** Customers want the outcome but the actual feature set doesn't deliver
- **Founder-specific:** The product only works because of a founder's unique network or expertise (not scalable)
- **Unit economics don't pencil:** CAC is too high, churn is too high, or margin is too low

**Example Failure Case:**

Same expense management product.

**Single point of failure:** "Mid-market companies already have Expensify or Brex. Switching cost is high because integrations are baked in. We can't acquire customers at <$6K CAC, which breaks the unit economics (LTV only $12K at 8% churn)."

**What breaks first:** Acquisition cost exceeds lifetime value. Money runs out before product finds legs.

## Building Scenarios

### Step 1: Identify the Biggest Assumptions

List the three most uncertain numbers:

1. **Churn rate** — Do customers stick, or is this a one-time purchase?
2. **CAC** — Can we acquire customers profitably?
3. **Price acceptance** — Will customers actually pay what we think?

### Step 2: Vary One Assumption Per Scenario

**Best case:** Churn is 2% (half expected), others are assumed as planned.
**Base case:** All assumptions as estimated.
**Failure case:** Churn is 10% (double expected), or CAC is 2x, or price acceptance is 50%.

### Step 3: Calculate the Outcome

For each scenario, run the unit economics and project Year 1–3:

| Metric | Best | Base | Failure |
|--------|------|------|---------|
| Churn (monthly) | 2% | 5% | 10% |
| CAC | $2K | $4K | $6K |
| Price | $99 | $99 | $99 |
| LTV | $45K | $18K | $9K |
| LTV:CAC | 22.5x | 4.5x | 1.5x |
| Year 3 ARR | $3.5M | $2M | <$500K (likely dead) |

### Step 4: Identify Inflection Points

Where does each scenario diverge?

- **Best vs. Base:** Usually Year 2–3, when compounding matters
- **Base vs. Failure:** Usually in unit economics ratios; failure case shows LTV:CAC < 3x by Year 2

### Step 5: Name the Assumption You'll Test First

Best case assumes churn = 2%. That's unverified.
Your first 30 days: Talk to 10 customers. What's the actual churn risk?

## Scenario Pitfalls

### Mistake 1: Best Case Is "Overnight Success"

❌ "We get 1,000 customers in Year 1."
✓ "We get 40 customers in Year 1, by focusing on one vertical and two sales channels."

Best case is still credible. It's not fantasy. It's excellence, not luck.

### Mistake 2: Base Case Is Too Optimistic

❌ Base case has churn = 2%. But industry benchmark is 6%. You're assuming you're exceptional from day one.
✓ Base case has churn = 5%, with a narrative for why it could be lower (strong onboarding, stickiness built in).

Base case should feel achievable with good execution, not lucky.

### Mistake 3: Failure Case Is Vague

❌ "Maybe the market isn't ready."
✓ "If churn exceeds 8% (because we can't make the product sticky with mid-market workflows), LTV drops below $10K, CAC is $6K, and we can't grow profitably."

Name the specific failure. Make it testable.

### Mistake 4: Forgetting Unit Economics in Scenarios

❌ "Year 1: 10 customers. Year 2: 50 customers."
✓ "Year 1: 10 customers, $120K ARR, $20K CAC = $200K spend. We need $400K to survive. Burn rate = 50% per year."

Connect scenarios to financial viability. If LTV:CAC < 3x, growth is a treadmill, not an exit.

---

## The Litmus Test

After scenarios, ask:

1. **Best case:** Is this credible? Could a smart team execute this?
2. **Base case:** Would I personally be excited to build this for 3 years, even if it becomes "good but not great"?
3. **Failure case:** What would you do if this happens? Have a Plan B, or is the business dead?

If you can't find a credible best case, narrow the idea further.
If base case doesn't excite you, consider a different idea.
If failure case seems too likely, don't start yet. Validate first.
